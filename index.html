Lotus Temple

The Lotus Temple, a Baháʼí House of Worship in New Delhi, resembles a lotus flower and was inaugurated in 1986. With 27 marble "petals" forming its structure, it welcomes all, regardless of faith, and has won multiple architectural awards.

History

The Lotus Temple, inaugurated in 1986 in New Delhi, India, is a Baháʼí House of Worship renowned for its lotus-like design. Designed by Fariborz Sahba, it symbolizes purity and unity. Open to all regardless of religion, it has become a significant landmark, attracting visitors worldwide. Its architectural excellence has earned it numerous awards and widespread recognition in the global architectural community.

Vision

The vision of the Lotus Temple is to serve as a sanctuary of unity, where people of all backgrounds can come together in worship and reflection. It embodies the Bahá'í principle of oneness, fostering understanding and harmony among diverse cultures and religions. Through its serene environment and inclusive ethos, it aims to inspire spiritual renewal and collective efforts towards peace and social progress. The temple stands as a beacon of hope, promoting the ideals of love, tolerance, and unity in a world often divided by differences.

Goals

The Lotus Temple aims to foster interfaith dialogue and understanding among people of different religions and cultures. It strives to promote the principles of unity, peace, and harmony as espoused by the Bahá'í Faith. Through its open-door policy, it seeks to create an inclusive space where all individuals can come together in prayer and meditation.
